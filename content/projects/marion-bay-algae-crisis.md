## The Brief

When South Australia faced one of its worst toxic algae blooms on record — lasting over six months and devastating marine ecosystems — InkHunter responded the only way he knew how: with art. This was not a commission but a calling. Patrick travelled 16,000 km to Marion Bay to donate his time, skills, and materials to a community in crisis.

## Community Engagement

The project was supported by local artists, the Marion Bay community, and the Great Southern Reef Foundation. Together, they channelled their frustration, grief, and determination into a work that would amplify the crisis to a national audience.

## Process

The mural design takes the form of an underwater "coat of arms" — a formal, heraldic composition featuring two of the marine species most threatened by the algae crisis: the rare Leafy Sea Dragon and the Giant Australian Cuttlefish. These creatures, found nowhere else on Earth, became symbols of what South Australia stood to lose.

The work was accompanied by a public call for the marine crisis to be declared a natural disaster — using the power of visual storytelling to demand political action.

## Outcome

The Marion Bay mural generated coverage in The Wire and other outlets, bringing national attention to a crisis that had received insufficient media coverage. The Great Southern Reef Foundation cited the artwork as a significant contribution to their advocacy efforts, and the mural became a gathering point for the Marion Bay community.
