## The Brief

The City of Parramatta, supported by a $3.5 million Australian Government investment in the Harris Park Cultural Precinct, commissioned InkHunter to create the largest mural in Parramatta — transforming Station Street East into a vibrant cultural landmark that positions Harris Park's Little India as a must-visit destination.

The artwork needed to honour the area's deep Indian-Australian heritage while celebrating the broader multicultural tapestry of the precinct — a place where food, festivals, migration stories, and community life converge.

## Community Engagement

The mural was shaped through extensive community consultation reflecting the voices and lived experiences of Harris Park residents. Working alongside co-artists Gauri Torgalkar and Em Hatton, InkHunter facilitated workshops with local business owners, cultural organisations, long-time residents, and recent arrivals to ensure every metre of the wall told an authentic story.

Key themes emerged through consultation: the journey of migration, the warmth of shared meals, the colours of Diwali and Holi, the flow of the Parramatta River connecting all communities, and the native wildlife that has always called this place home.

## Process

The 100+ metre mural unfolds as a narrative journey from sunrise to nightfall along Station Street East. Working with a team of artists, InkHunter developed a visual language that weaves Indian and Australian elements — lotus flowers meeting native banksias, peacocks alongside rosellas, traditional patterns flowing into river currents.

Sustainable Colormaker paints were used throughout, continuing InkHunter's commitment to environmentally responsible materials.

## Outcome

The Harris Park Cultural Precinct mural has become a landmark destination, drawing visitors from across Sydney and positioning Little India as a premier cultural precinct. Media coverage spanned national outlets, and local businesses reported increased foot traffic within the first month of completion.

The project demonstrates how large-scale public art can serve as a catalyst for urban renewal, cultural celebration, and community pride — all within a framework of genuine consultation and emerging artist collaboration.
