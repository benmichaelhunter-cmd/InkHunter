## The Brief

The Australian National Maritime Museum sought a large-scale artwork for their front entrance that would engage visitors before they even stepped inside — something that captured the wonder and fragility of Australia's marine environment while pushing the boundaries of what public art could do.

## Community Engagement

Shallow Iridescence was built through a collaborative, layered process. InkHunter worked alongside First Nations curator Tyson Frigo, artists Keith Stanley, Em Hatton, John Foley, and Colin McKinnon to create a baseline grid of symbols and patterns that reflected diverse perspectives on Australia's relationship with the ocean.

## Process

The mural was designed as a perspective artwork — its full meaning reveals itself depending on where you stand. From the museum entrance, visitors see a vibrant underwater tableau of marine life and symbols. But step back onto Pyrmont Bridge, and a giant blue-ringed octopus emerges from the patterns — beautiful, dangerous, and demanding attention.

This technique transforms the entire museum forecourt into a single, living artwork that rewards curiosity and movement.

## Outcome

Shallow Iridescence led directly to the commission of "Octopus Garden" — an immersive digital experience created in collaboration with Junior Major for the museum's interior. The mural generated significant media coverage and established a new model for how museums can use public art to extend their storytelling beyond gallery walls.
