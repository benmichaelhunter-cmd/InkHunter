## Decoration vs. Belonging

There's a mural near my studio that was painted by a talented artist. Beautiful technique, striking colours. Nobody cares about it. It gets tagged regularly. No one cleans it up.

A few suburbs over, there's a simpler mural — technically less impressive — that the local community treats like a landmark. Kids point it out from car windows. The cafe next door named a menu item after it. It's never been vandalised.

The difference isn't quality. It's ownership.

## How We Run Consultation

At InkHunter, community consultation isn't a box-ticking exercise. It's the creative engine of the project. Here's what that actually looks like:

**Phase 1: Listening.** Before we sketch a single line, we spend time in the neighbourhood. We talk to shop owners, attend community events, sit in parks and watch how people use the space. We're not looking for design briefs — we're absorbing the identity of a place.

**Phase 2: Workshops.** We run structured sessions where residents contribute stories, images, and ideas. In Harris Park, over 200 people participated. In Maitland, elders shared family histories that became central to the mural's narrative.

**Phase 3: Translation.** This is the artist's job — taking hundreds of inputs and finding the visual thread that connects them. Not everything can make it onto the wall, but every contribution shapes the language and feeling of the final work.

**Phase 4: Feedback.** We share design concepts with the community before painting begins. Not for approval — for resonance. Does this feel like your place? Does this tell your story?

## The Vandalism Test

Here's a metric that matters: community-consulted murals almost never get vandalised. When people see themselves in a piece of public art, they protect it. That alone should convince every council to invest in proper consultation.

## It Takes Longer. It's Worth It.

Consultation adds weeks to a project timeline and complexity to the budget. But the result is public art that actually serves its public — art that strengthens community bonds, attracts visitors, and endures because the people who live with it want it to endure.
