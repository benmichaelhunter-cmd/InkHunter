## The Phone Call

I first heard about Marion Bay's algal bloom crisis through a news article. Toxic algae had smothered the bay, killing marine life on a devastating scale. Giant cuttlefish, weedy sea dragons, fish, crustaceans — entire ecosystems wiped out. The local community was heartbroken and felt abandoned.

I picked up the phone and called the Marion Bay Progress Association. "I'm a mural artist from Sydney," I said. "I'd like to come down and paint something for your town. No charge."

There was a pause. Then: "When can you get here?"

## The Drive

Sydney to Marion Bay is roughly 1,600 kilometres. I loaded the van with Colormaker sustainable paints, brushes, and enough supplies for a week of painting. The drive took two days through some of Australia's most beautiful and most harsh landscape.

Arriving in Marion Bay, the scale of the environmental damage was immediately visible. The smell of decaying algae hung in the air. Locals pointed to stretches of coastline that had been teeming with life just months earlier.

## Painting the Crisis

The mural depicts the giant cuttlefish and weedy sea dragon — two iconic species that defined Marion Bay's marine identity. I wanted the image to serve as both a memorial and a rallying cry. These creatures were here. They mattered. Their loss should not be quietly accepted.

Working in the South Australian heat, painting on a community-donated wall, surrounded by locals who stopped by with cold drinks and stories about what the bay used to look like — it was one of the most meaningful experiences of my career.

## Why Pro Bono Matters

Not every project needs to be paid. Some stories need to be told urgently, and waiting for a council grant or corporate sponsor means waiting too long. Marion Bay's community needed to know that someone outside their postcode cared about what was happening to their home.

The mural is now a local landmark. It's been featured in Australian Rural & Regional News and The Wire. More importantly, it's given the community a focal point for their advocacy — something visible and permanent that says: this happened here, and it matters.

## Art as First Response

Environmental crises are usually met with scientific reports and policy submissions. Those matter. But a mural on the side of a building does something different — it makes the crisis visible to everyone who passes by, every single day. It doesn't let people forget.

That's the role art can play in environmental advocacy. Not as a replacement for science and policy, but as the thing that keeps the conversation alive when the news cycle moves on.
