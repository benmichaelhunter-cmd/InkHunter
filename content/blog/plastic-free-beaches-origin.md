## A Bucket of Bottle Caps

It started with a morning surf at Dee Why. I came out of the water and found a stretch of sand littered with bottle caps, chip packets, and fragments of polystyrene. I filled an entire bucket in ten minutes.

I'd been doing beach clean-ups for years, but that morning something clicked: we were treating the symptoms, not the cause. No amount of volunteer clean-ups would fix a system designed to produce disposable plastic at industrial scale.

## Street Art as Accountability

I went home and started designing posters. Not polite awareness campaigns — confrontational images that named the brands responsible. Coca-Cola. Nestle. McDonald's. Each poster featured their logo reimagined in the context of the pollution their packaging creates.

I printed them on my kitchen table using water-based inks on recycled paper, then spent a week wheat-pasting them across the Northern Beaches. Manly, Dee Why, Curl Curl, Freshwater — anywhere people walked to the beach, the posters were there.

## When the Media Called

Within days, the images were circulating on social media. Then 7 News picked up the story. Suddenly I was standing on a beach explaining to a national television audience why street art was a valid form of environmental activism.

The segment went to air and the response was overwhelming. Councils reached out. Schools wanted to run workshops. Other artists asked how they could help.

## What It Taught Me

Plastic Free Beaches proved something I'd suspected but never tested at scale: art cuts through the noise in a way that data alone cannot. People had seen the statistics about ocean plastic a hundred times. But a poster showing a sea turtle wrapped in branded packaging — that stopped people in their tracks.

It became the foundation for everything InkHunter does today. Every mural, every community project, every collaboration starts from the same principle: make the invisible visible, and people will act.
