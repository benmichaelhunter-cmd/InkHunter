## The Problem with Conventional Paint

Most commercial paints are petroleum-based. The production of a single litre of conventional paint generates roughly 10 kilograms of CO2, and that's before you factor in volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that off-gas for months after application. When you're painting a mural that covers 100+ square metres, those numbers add up fast.

As someone whose entire practice is built around environmental advocacy, painting with petrochemical products always felt like a contradiction I needed to resolve.

## Discovering Colormaker

I first encountered Colormaker Industries through a sustainability expo in Sydney. Their paints are manufactured using renewable, bio-based binders derived from algae and plant oils. The colour range is extraordinary — vivid, durable, and UV-resistant enough for the harsh Australian climate.

After testing on smaller projects, I was convinced. The coverage is equal to or better than conventional equivalents, and the paints cure to a finish that withstands coastal salt air and Western Sydney heat alike.

## It's Not Just About the Paint

Switching materials was the easy part. The harder shift was rethinking the entire supply chain — from primer to sealant to cleaning solvents. Every product that touches the wall now meets the same sustainability criteria.

## The Marion Bay Test

The real proof came at Marion Bay in South Australia, where I donated my time painting a mural about the algal bloom crisis devastating local marine life. Using algae-based paint to depict the destruction caused by algal blooms — there's a poetic circularity to that which wasn't lost on the community.

The mural has now weathered a full South Australian summer with no fading or degradation.

## Making It the Standard

My goal is to prove that sustainable materials aren't a compromise — they're an upgrade. Every project is a data point. Every mural that still looks vibrant after years of Australian sun is evidence that the industry can and should change.
