## From Blank Wall to Cultural Landmark

When we first stood in front of 100+ metres of bare wall on Station Street East, the scale was daunting. But the stories we'd collected through months of community consultation — stories of migration, celebration, food, family, and belonging — filled every centimetre with purpose.

## The Consultation Process

Working alongside Gauri Torgalkar and Em Hatton, we facilitated workshops with over 200 Harris Park residents. Business owners shared stories of opening their first restaurant. Families described Diwali celebrations that spilled into the streets. Children drew their favourite animals from the Parramatta River.

These weren't abstract design exercises — they were genuine conversations that shaped every element of the mural.

## Finding the Visual Language

The challenge was weaving Indian and Australian visual traditions into a single, cohesive narrative. We developed a flowing style where lotus flowers naturally transition into native banksias, where traditional rangoli patterns merge with the geometry of river currents.

The mural reads as a journey from sunrise to nightfall — mirroring the daily rhythm of Harris Park, from morning chai to evening celebrations.

## Sustainable Practice

As with all InkHunter projects, we used Colormaker sustainable paints throughout. On a project of this scale, the environmental impact of material choices is significant. It's a non-negotiable part of how we work.

## What It Means

The Harris Park Cultural Precinct mural isn't just decoration — it's a declaration. It says this community matters, these stories deserve to be seen, and this place has a cultural identity worth celebrating at the largest possible scale.
